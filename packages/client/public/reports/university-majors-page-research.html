Stanford University

bulletin.stanford.edu/programs  —  349 programs listed

Best Filtering UX Custom Illustrations

Layout

Two-column layout with a deep filter sidebar on the left and card-based results on the right. Pagination at the bottom handles 349 total programs across multiple pages. Clean, utility-forward design.

Navigation & Filtering

The most sophisticated filter system of any university reviewed. Eight distinct filter dimensions:

  • Search bar with typeahead ("Type to search programs...")
  • Program Level: Undergraduate / Graduate radio buttons
  • Academic Areas: Dropdown with autocomplete
  • Interests: Dropdown with autocomplete
  • Degree: Dropdown with autocomplete
  • Coterm Masters Programs: Yes / No toggle
  • Academic Options: Minor, PhD Minor, Departmental Honors checkboxes
  • Department(s): Dropdown with autocomplete

Results count displayed live ("Results (349)") and sort options include Catalog Display Name, Degree Designation, and Description.

Program Cards

Full-width horizontal cards with custom department illustrations (not photos) on the left, program name with degree designation (e.g., "Aeronautics and Astronautics (BS)"), a 2–4 sentence outcome-oriented description, and a right-arrow link to the detail page. Each degree variant gets its own card.

Strengths
Gold-standard filtering. Typeahead search. Custom illustrations add visual identity. Outcome-oriented copy per program.
Weaknesses
No Apply/Visit CTAs on the listing page. No trust stats visible. Pagination over infinite scroll may slow exploration.

MIT

catalog.mit.edu/subjects  —  40+ subject areas

Dual Index System Reference-Only

Layout

Academic catalog format with a deep hierarchical left sidebar and three tabbed views: Overview (alphabetical), By Course Number (MIT's numerical system), and a Key tab explaining notation. Designed for current students and advisors, not prospective student marketing.

Navigation & Filtering

Catalog-wide search bar. Two indexing methods: alphabetical and by MIT's unique course numbering system (Course 6 = EECS, Course 18 = Math). Left sidebar organized by schools with expandable sections.

Program Display

Simple bulleted hyperlink lists on the landing page. Each entry is "[Subject Name] (Course Number)" linking to a dedicated page. Detail pages show prerequisites, credit structure (lecture-lab-prep format), term offerings, requirement badges, instructor names, and full descriptions.

Strengths
Dual indexing is genuinely useful. Key tab teaches users the notation. Most information-dense detail pages of any school.
Weaknesses
Zero marketing value. No imagery, no stats, no CTAs. Would not work for enrollment-focused pages.

Duke University

admissions.duke.edu/academic-possibilities  —  147 programs (63 majors, 61 minors, 23 certificates)

Best Storytelling Best Conversion

Layout

Narrative storytelling page that reads as a journey. Hero carousel, then progressive disclosure: "The Duke Way of Thinking" philosophy, Academic Opportunities split by school (Trinity College, Pratt Engineering), full alphabetical lists, research section, and international opportunities.

Navigation & Filtering

No filters at all. Programs are presented as three flat alphabetical text lists (Majors, Minors, Certificates) with asterisk notations for restrictions. The page relies on storytelling to engage users rather than search/filter tooling.

Trust & Stats

The richest statistical content of any school reviewed:

  • 430,000 unique academic combinations available
  • 83% of students pursue more than one major
  • 53% undergrads graduate with research experience
  • $800M spent on Duke research each year
  • 200+ study abroad programs

Conversion

Persistent header CTAs (Connect, Visit, Apply) visible at all times. Mailing list signup in footer. "Learn More" arrows throughout. The most conversion-optimized page of all ten reviewed.

Strengths
Compelling narrative arc. Always-visible CTAs. Best use of statistics to sell outcomes. Hero carousel for visual engagement.
Weaknesses
No filtering on 147 programs. Flat text lists are hard to scan. Must scroll through entire page to find a specific program.

Northwestern University

northwestern.edu/academics  —  11 schools, multi-pathway exploration

Multi-Pathway UX School Grid Cards

Layout

Gateway page with three exploration pathways: by program type (Undergraduate, Graduate, Continuing), by school (11 visual cards), and by A-Z filterable data tables. Audience tabs at the top (Students, Faculty, Parents, Alumni, Media).

Navigation & Filtering

The Graduate A-Z page has a solid filterable table with three dimensions: filter by degree granted (Master's, Doctoral), by interest tags, and by Northwestern school. Results display as a four-column table (Program, School, Degree Type, Tags).

Program Display

Gateway uses image cards with thumbnails per school. A-Z pages use structured data tables. The 11-school grid with consistent card layouts provides visual browsing by institutional organization.

Strengths
Accommodates multiple user research patterns. School grid is visually browsable. Graduate A-Z table has real filtering. Experiential learning section.
Weaknesses
No persistent Apply CTAs. Gateway page is generic. Filtering only available on sub-pages, not the main landing.

University of Michigan (LSA)

lsa.umich.edu/lsa/academics/majors-minors.html  —  185+ programs

Best Badge Filtering Best Inline Preview

Layout

Audience selector bar, then quick-action buttons, breadcrumb, introductory text, two rows of color-coded filter buttons, a "Filtered by" status indicator, and a three-column interactive data table with inline accordion expansion.

Navigation & Filtering

The most visually intuitive filter system. Two rows of colored badge buttons:

  • Category: HUM (Humanities, dark red), NAT (Natural Sciences, green), SOC (Social Sciences, teal), INT (Interdisciplinary, teal)
  • Type: M (Major, dark green), m (Minor, red), sm (Sub-Major, gray), sps (Supplemental Studies)

Active filters display in a "Filtered by:" status bar. Programs show matching badge icons inline.

Program Display

Three-column data table (Name, Type, Category). Clicking a program expands an inline accordion showing: full description, related programs with cross-links, and three CTA buttons (Requirements, Website, Advising). Users can preview details without leaving the page.

Why this matters for Coe

Michigan handles 185+ programs without overwhelming the page by combining color-coded visual filtering with inline previews. This is the closest pattern to what would work at Coe's scale (60+ programs) — achievable without the engineering investment of Stanford's system.

Strengths
Color-coded badges are instantly scannable. Inline accordion eliminates page navigation. Three CTAs per expanded program. Career outcomes link.
Weaknesses
No text search. Badge labels (HUM, NAT, SOC) require learning. No images or visual content per program.

Harvard University

college.harvard.edu/academics/fields-study  —  Editorial gateway (no directory)

Brand-First No Program List

Layout

This is deliberately NOT a program directory. It's an editorial gateway page with a video hero, long-form narrative about Harvard's liberal arts philosophy, quotes from the president and dean, expandable accordions for concentration categories, and a "View all Concentrations" link to a separate directory.

Approach

Harvard uses brand storytelling over utility. Stats are woven into narrative: "3,700+ courses in 50 undergraduate fields," "About half of all students choose honors track." The page prioritizes philosophy and prestige over program discovery.

Strengths
Premium brand feel. Video hero. Authoritative leadership quotes. "On This Page" quick navigation for long content.
Weaknesses
A prospective student asking "What can I major in?" must click through to a separate page. Zero filtering or quick-scan capability. Only works because it's Harvard.

Yale University

admissions.yale.edu/majors-and-academic-programs  —  80+ majors

AI Chatbot Student Stories

Layout

Hero with heading and lab photo, editorial intro about open curriculum, flat alphabetical list of 80+ hyperlinked major names, 4-stat callout section, student blog posts with thumbnails, dual-degree programs, and an AI chat launcher that appears multiple times.

Navigation & Filtering

None. 80+ programs presented as one continuous alphabetical text list with no search, filters, or A-Z jump links. The simplest possible format.

Unique Features

  • AI-powered admissions chatbot ("Ask A Question") — the most innovative conversion tool of any school. Appears 3+ times on the page.
  • Student blog integration: "Filming My Senior Thesis at Yale," "Pre-Med Life at Yale," "When Your Professor Wins the Nobel Prize"
  • Interdisciplinary "and" branding: Computer Science and Economics, Mathematics & Philosophy — tied to Yale's "Embrace And" platform
Strengths
AI chatbot addresses real student questions. Blog post integration adds student voice. Strong statistical proof points (2,000+ courses, 20% double major).
Weaknesses
Zero filtering on 80+ programs. No descriptions per major. Must click through for any detail.

Princeton University

princeton.edu/academics/areas-of-study  —  200+ programs

Best Filtering (Ivy) Most Info Per Program

Layout

Clean utility page with a search/filter bar at top and alphabetical accordion-style program listings below. The most functionally rich page among the Ivies.

Navigation & Filtering

  • Keyword search: Real-time program lookup
  • Degree type dropdown: Undergraduate Degree, Certificate, Minor, Graduate Degree, Graduate Certificate

Program Display

Collapsible accordion items. Clicking a program expands to reveal: full description (50-100 words), "View Courses" link, Academic Units with internal links, and Degrees/Certificates/Minors with clickable degree type links (A.B., Ph.D., M.S.E., M.Eng.). The richest per-program information of any school reviewed.

Strengths
Real search + degree type filter. Accordion handles 200+ programs without overwhelm. Richest detail per program. Clean information architecture.
Weaknesses
Purely text-based, no images. No student stories or outcome data. Utilitarian with zero brand storytelling.

Williams College

williams.edu/academics/areas-of-study  —  ~55 programs

"What's Offered" Column Minimal

Layout

Hero image, editorial intro explaining majors vs. concentrations, then a two-column HTML table with "Area of Study" and "What's Offered" columns. Right sidebar with Academics section navigation. Print-friendly.

Program Display

Static two-column table. Column 1 links to the department site. Column 2 shows "Major," "Concentration," or "More information." The "What's Offered" column is a genuinely useful UX element that instantly distinguishes program types.

Strengths
Extremely clean and scannable. "What's Offered" column solves a real UX problem. Zero cognitive load. References to unique programs (Williams-Exeter at Oxford).
Weaknesses
No filtering, no descriptions, no imagery, no stats. Purely functional and text-only. Feels dated.

Amherst College

amherst.edu/academiclife/departments  —  43 majors

Best Stat Cards Open Curriculum

Layout

Left sidebar with Academics navigation, then four colored statistic cards as the hero element, intro paragraph, three-column bulleted list of 43 hyperlinked majors, supplementary sections (Interdisciplinary Majors, Creative Writing, Five College Programs), and course catalog buttons.

Stat Cards (Hero)

The best trust signal device of any school reviewed. Four colored cards displayed immediately:

  • 43 — Number of majors (green)
  • 7:1 — Student-to-faculty ratio, 209 full-time instructional faculty (maroon)
  • 19 — Average class size, 84% under 30 students (teal)
  • 850+ — Courses, 6,000+ through Five College Consortium (dark blue)

Why this matters for Coe

Amherst is the closest peer school in size and type to Coe (small liberal arts, ~43 vs ~60 programs). Their stat cards approach is directly applicable — and the Coe mockup already includes this pattern with the trust strip and outcomes section.

Strengths
Stat cards are immediately impactful. Three-column layout is efficient. Anticipates common questions (Creative Writing, interdisciplinary options, Five College access).
Weaknesses
No filtering, no descriptions per major. CMS taxonomy tags exposed to users. Page feels dated. No imagery per program.

Key Takeaways for Coe

What the best schools do — and where the Coe mockup already leads

Takeaway 01

The Coe mockup is ahead of most Ivies

The proposed Coe redesign already includes search, filter pills, visual cards with descriptions, trust stats, outcomes data, and Schema.org markup. Harvard, Yale, Williams, and Amherst have none of these. The mockup's card-based grid with filter pills is more functional than 7 of the 10 schools reviewed.

Takeaway 02

Steal Michigan's inline accordion pattern

The "View all 60 programs" button could expand into a filterable list with inline accordions (like UMich) rather than navigating to a new page. This lets users preview program details without leaving the page — reducing bounce and increasing engagement.

Takeaway 03

Add degree-type badges to cards (like Williams)

Williams' "What's Offered" column is a small UX win that instantly communicates Major vs. Minor vs. Concentration. The Coe mockup already has tags (Major, Minor, New) — consider expanding these to be filterable and adding them to all programs.

Takeaway 04

Keep the conversion CTAs — Duke proves it works

Duke is the only top school with persistent Apply/Visit/Connect CTAs. The Coe mockup's "Apply Now" header button and quiz CTA are smart. Consider adding a "Request Info" or "Plan a Visit" secondary CTA alongside it.

Takeaway 05

The quiz CTA is a differentiator

No other school in this research has an on-page "Find Your Major" quiz. Yale's AI chatbot is the closest parallel. This is a genuine competitive advantage for Coe — a small school can offer personalized guidance that large universities cannot.

Takeaway 06

Schema.org markup is a competitive edge

Only 3 of 10 schools (Duke, Harvard, and Stanford partially) use structured data. The Coe mockup's three JSON-LD blocks (CollegeOrUniversity, BreadcrumbList, ItemList with EducationalOccupationalProgram) are best-in-class and will improve search visibility.

Takeaway 07

No one shows salary/career data on the listing page

The Coe mockup's outcomes section ($48K avg salary, 97% employed, 85% internship) is unique among all 10 schools. For ROI-minded families choosing between schools, this is a strong selling point. Keep it prominent.

Takeaway 08

Make filters actually work

The mockup's filter pills (All, Majors, Minors, Pre-Professional, STEM, Business, Arts & Humanities) are the right categories. In implementation, combine text search with pill filters — this matches the UMich badge approach and Stanford's multi-dimensional filtering at a scale appropriate for 60 programs.
